Driving While License Suspended Cases in Judge Myers' Courtroom

42% of people charged with Driving While License Suspended With Knowledge are convicted. 24% of cases are dismissed, and 34% of people receive a withhold of adjudication, meaning that while they may receive a penalty for the crime, they do not receive a conviction on their record.

Do people hire an attorney for Driving While License Suspended cases in Judge Myers's courtroom?

Only 14% of people charged with Driving While License Suspended With Knowledge hire a private attorney in Judge Myers' courtroom. 17% go with the Public Defender, while 69% decide to handle their cases on their own, which is called "Pro Se."

Does hiring a private attorney make a difference?

Having a private attorney is an advantage in Judge Myers' courtroom for these cases. People who hire a private attorney are convicted 34% of the time, while people who have the public defender are convicted 60% of the time. People without an attorney, also known as "Pro Se," are convicted 38% of the time.

Across Hillsborough County people who hired a private attorney are convicted of Driving While License Suspended With Knowledge 43% of the time, while people represented by the public defender are convicted 69% of the time. Can I get my Driving While License Suspended case dismissed or dropped?

People who hire a private attorney have their cases dismissed 32% of the time, while people who have the public defender had their cases dismissed 24% of the time. People without an attorney, also known as "Pro Se," have their case dismissed 23% of the time.

What about getting a Withhold of Adjudication?

People who hire a private attorney receive a withhold of adjudication 34% of the time, while people with the public defender receive a withhold of adjudication 16% of the time. People without an attorney receive a withhold of adjudication 39% of the time.

What is the typical penalty for Driving While License Suspended?

The most common penalty for Driving While License Suspended with Knowledge is a $270 fine. Less than 1% of people receive probation for this charge.
